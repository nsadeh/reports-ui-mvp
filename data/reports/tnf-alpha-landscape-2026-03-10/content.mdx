# Executive Summary

The TNF-alpha market access landscape is undergoing its most significant transformation since the originator biologics established dominance over a decade ago. Biosimilar penetration has accelerated sharply through Q1 2026, driven by interchangeability designations, aggressive payer formulary actions, and continued pricing pressure from manufacturers seeking preferred positioning.

This report covers the five major TNF-alpha originators — adalimumab (Humira), etanercept (Enbrel), infliximab (Remicade), certolizumab pegol (Cimzia), and golimumab (Simponi) — and their biosimilar competitors across the key indications of rheumatoid arthritis, plaque psoriasis, and inflammatory bowel disease.

Key findings from this period:

1. **Biosimilar-first formulary policies are now the norm.** Four of the five largest commercial payers have moved at least one adalimumab biosimilar to preferred status, with Humira relegated to non-preferred or prior authorization tiers. This shift, which began in mid-2024, has accelerated through early 2026.

2. **Interchangeability is reshaping step therapy.** The FDA's interchangeability designations for Cyltezo and Hadlima have given payers regulatory cover to mandate biosimilar starts in treatment-naive patients. Step-through-originator requirements are disappearing for adalimumab in RA and psoriasis.

3. **The infliximab market tells a different story.** Unlike adalimumab, infliximab biosimilar adoption has been complicated by the IV administration model and site-of-care economics. Hospital outpatient departments and infusion centers have been slower to switch, and payer formulary actions have been less aggressive.

4. **Novel TNF-alpha entrants face a challenging access environment.** Any new molecular entity targeting TNF-alpha would need to demonstrate meaningful clinical differentiation to secure formulary placement above the biosimilar tier. The pricing ceiling has dropped substantially.

# Target Overview

Tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-alpha) is a pro-inflammatory cytokine central to the pathogenesis of multiple autoimmune and inflammatory conditions. TNF-alpha inhibitors represent one of the largest therapeutic classes in specialty pharmacy, with combined U.S. spending exceeding $40 billion annually at peak — a figure now declining as biosimilar competition intensifies.

The approved originator landscape includes five distinct molecules: adalimumab (Humira, AbbVie), etanercept (Enbrel, Amgen), infliximab (Remicade, Janssen), certolizumab pegol (Cimzia, UCB), and golimumab (Simponi/Simponi Aria, Janssen). Adalimumab and etanercept are subcutaneous self-injectable biologics dominating the outpatient setting. Infliximab is primarily IV-administered, while certolizumab and golimumab occupy smaller market shares with differentiated clinical profiles.

The biosimilar wave has been most impactful for adalimumab, with 10+ approved biosimilars now on the U.S. market, and for infliximab, with 5 approved biosimilars. Etanercept biosimilars have seen more modest uptake. Certolizumab and golimumab do not yet face biosimilar competition.

# Indication Deep-Dive: Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis remains the largest indication by volume for TNF-alpha inhibitors and the primary battleground for biosimilar competition. Approximately 60% of all TNF-alpha prescriptions in the U.S. are written for RA, making payer formulary decisions in this indication the single most impactful variable in the market access landscape.

## Standard of Care and Positioning

TNF-alpha inhibitors are established as second-line therapy for moderate-to-severe RA after inadequate response to conventional DMARDs (typically methotrexate). The 2025 ACR guidelines continue to recommend TNF-alpha inhibitors as a first-line biologic option alongside JAK inhibitors and IL-6 inhibitors, though the guidelines now explicitly note that biosimilars should be considered equivalent to their reference products.

## Payer Access Patterns

The major commercial payers have converged on a biosimilar-preferred strategy for adalimumab in RA:

- **Aetna:** Cyltezo and Hadlima are preferred. Humira requires prior authorization and documented biosimilar failure or contraindication. Step therapy through a preferred biosimilar is required for treatment-naive patients.
- **UnitedHealthcare:** Hyrimoz is the sole preferred adalimumab product. All other adalimumab products (including Humira) require step through Hyrimoz.
- **Cigna:** Cyltezo preferred. Hadlima and Hyrimoz are non-preferred but accessible via PA. Humira is excluded on most Cigna commercial plans as of January 2026.
- **Anthem/Elevance:** Hadlima preferred. Cyltezo is non-preferred tier. Humira available via medical exception only.
- **Humana:** Cyltezo and Hyrimoz are co-preferred. Humira is non-preferred with step therapy requirement.

## Step Therapy and Prior Authorization

All five major commercial payers now require prior authorization for originator Humira in RA. Four of five require step therapy through at least one preferred biosimilar before authorizing Humira. The exception is Humana, which allows Humira access via PA without mandatory biosimilar trial, though the non-preferred copay differential strongly incentivizes biosimilar use.

For treatment-naive RA patients starting a TNF-alpha inhibitor, the practical access path at most commercial plans is now: methotrexate trial → preferred adalimumab biosimilar. The step-through-Humira paradigm has been inverted.

## Clinical Evidence Thresholds

Payers evaluating new TNF-alpha products for RA access are benchmarking against established efficacy endpoints: ACR20 response at week 12 (minimum ~60%), ACR50 at week 24 (minimum ~35%), and DAS28-CRP remission rates. Safety profiles are compared against the extensive adalimumab post-marketing database. Any new entrant would need to demonstrate non-inferiority at minimum, and meaningful superiority to justify formulary placement above the biosimilar tier.

# Indication Deep-Dive: Plaque Psoriasis

Plaque psoriasis represents the second-largest indication for TNF-alpha inhibitors, though the competitive landscape here is more complex due to the rise of IL-17 and IL-23 inhibitors as preferred biologics.

## Standard of Care and Positioning

TNF-alpha inhibitors have been displaced from first-line biologic status in moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis by IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab, ixekizumab) and IL-23 inhibitors (guselkumab, risankizumab, tildrakizumab). The 2025 AAD-NPF guidelines recommend IL-17 or IL-23 inhibitors as preferred first-line biologics for PsO, with TNF-alpha inhibitors positioned as alternatives.

However, TNF-alpha inhibitors retain relevance for patients with concomitant psoriatic arthritis (where they remain first-line) and for patients who fail or are contraindicated for IL-17/IL-23 therapy.

## Payer Access Patterns

In psoriasis, payer formulary strategies are more fragmented than in RA:

- **Aetna:** Prefers IL-23 inhibitors (Skyrizi) as first-line biologic for PsO. TNF-alpha biosimilars are accessible as second-line without step through an originator.
- **UnitedHealthcare:** Cosentyx (IL-17) is preferred first-line biologic for PsO. TNF-alpha products are accessible but require documentation of IL-17 trial or contraindication.
- **Cigna:** Allows TNF-alpha biosimilars as first-line biologic for PsO alongside IL-17/IL-23 products. No mandatory step therapy sequence between classes.

## Biosimilar Impact

Biosimilar competition in PsO has been less impactful than in RA, primarily because TNF-alpha inhibitors are already second-line and prescription volumes are lower. However, when a TNF-alpha product is prescribed for PsO, the same biosimilar-preferred formulary rules apply — payers are not maintaining originator access in PsO when they've already removed it in RA.

# Indication Deep-Dive: Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis) represents a critical indication for TNF-alpha inhibitors, particularly infliximab and adalimumab. The market access dynamics here are distinct from RA and PsO due to the IV administration model for infliximab and the severity profile of the patient population.

## Standard of Care and Positioning

TNF-alpha inhibitors remain first-line biologic therapy for moderate-to-severe Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis. Unlike PsO, no alternative biologic class has displaced TNF-alpha in IBD. Vedolizumab (Entyvio, gut-selective integrin inhibitor) and ustekinumab (Stelara, IL-12/23) are established alternatives, but TNF-alpha inhibitors retain the deepest evidence base and broadest payer acceptance for first-line use.

## Payer Access Patterns

IBD formulary management differs from RA in several important ways:

- **Medical vs. pharmacy benefit:** Infliximab is typically billed under the medical benefit (J-code), while adalimumab is billed under the pharmacy benefit. This split creates different management levers for payers.
- **Site-of-care management:** Payers have implemented site-of-care programs directing infliximab infusions to lower-cost settings (home infusion, ambulatory infusion centers vs. hospital outpatient departments). This has been a more impactful cost lever than biosimilar substitution for infliximab.
- **Less aggressive biosimilar mandates:** In IBD, payers have been more cautious about mandating biosimilar switches for stable patients, reflecting gastroenterologist concerns about immunogenicity risk with switching in IBD (though clinical evidence does not strongly support these concerns).

## Key Payer Policies

- **Aetna:** Infliximab biosimilars (Inflectra, Renflexis) are preferred on medical benefit. New starts require biosimilar. Stable patients on Remicade can continue but face higher cost-sharing.
- **UnitedHealthcare:** Requires biosimilar infliximab for new starts in IBD. Has implemented a site-of-care program requiring home infusion for stable patients where clinically appropriate.
- **Cigna:** Adalimumab biosimilars preferred for IBD (same formulary as RA). Infliximab biosimilar preference varies by plan.

# Competitive Intelligence Highlights

## 1. Cyltezo Interchangeability Driving Market Share

Boehringer Ingelheim's Cyltezo (adalimumab-adbm) has reached an estimated 18% share of the adalimumab market in Q1 2026, up from 11% in Q3 2025. The interchangeability designation, combined with aggressive contracting and a citrate-free high-concentration formulation, has made Cyltezo the preferred adalimumab product at two of the five largest commercial payers.

## 2. Cigna Excludes Humira from Commercial Formularies

Effective January 1, 2026, Cigna's standard commercial formulary no longer includes Humira. Patients requiring adalimumab are directed to Cyltezo (preferred) or Hadlima/Hyrimoz (non-preferred). Medical exceptions for Humira require documented treatment failure on two biosimilars. This is the most aggressive anti-originator formulary action by a major payer to date.

## 3. Infliximab Biosimilar Uptake Lags Adalimumab

Despite preferred formulary positioning, infliximab biosimilars collectively hold only ~35% of the infliximab market, compared to ~55% biosimilar share for adalimumab. The gap is attributed to provider resistance in the infusion setting, buy-and-bill economics that previously favored Remicade, and slower adoption of site-of-care management programs.

## 4. AbbVie Launches Humira Patient Retention Programs

In response to biosimilar share erosion, AbbVie has expanded its Humira patient support programs, including copay elimination programs for commercially insured patients and enhanced nurse ambassador services. These programs are designed to reduce switching among stable patients but have had limited impact on new start share, which is now predominantly biosimilar.

## 5. JAK Inhibitor Safety Signal Creates TNF-alpha Tailwind

Ongoing post-marketing safety concerns with JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, upadacitinib, baricitinib) continue to benefit TNF-alpha inhibitor prescribing in RA. Several payers have tightened step therapy requirements for JAK inhibitors, requiring TNF-alpha failure before JAK access. This dynamic partially offsets the competitive pressure from IL-17/IL-23 inhibitors in the overall biologic market.
